Listen to a conversation between a student and a philosophy professor. 


Professor: Hi, Melissa, your rough draft for your paper is looking very promising so far. 


Student: Thanks, I still have some reading to do. Post-modernism is pretty challenging. So… 


Professor: Well, you are off to a good start. So anything else about the class or post-modernism, since we have a few minutes before my next appointment? 


Student: Well, actually, in class you talked about a French philosopher, Utah. You said he didn't believe in stories, or... or, something like that, that stories were invalid. 


Professor: I think I see, first of all, you understand what he... remember what Lyotard said about the Grand Narrative? 


Student: Not really. 


Professor: Ok, have a seat. 


Student: All right.


Professor: It's not quite the same way you are thinking of narratives, not stories. Lyotard meant narrative as in a way of understanding the world. 


Student: Um, I uh. 


Professor: How can I explain this... Grand Narrative… It's it's like an idea that helps people make sense of history. Like, when you picture the early middle ages in Europe, what do you think? 


Student: Ok, like, there were a lot of cultural achievements, then. Lots of wars, but not a lot of important art or books or anything. Like it was the dark ages.


Professor: All right. That's a simple explanation of a time in history, right? Something that tends to be accepted or understood by most people. That makes it a grand narrative. Here's a more recent one: Scientific progress. 


Student: Ah-ha.


Professor: People look at important inventions throughout history uh, light bulbs, cars, computers. We generally believe that there is an underlying universal truth that modern technology makes the world better. It's been the belief for so long that people accept it as being true. We don't even think of it much. We just accept that modern technology has that effect. 


Student: It does make our lives better. 


Professor: All right. This is where Lyotard comes in. He believed we have to be careful about accepting ideas like that. He said these beliefs really oversimplify things and that we should think more critically. Are there times when technology would not be considered progress where it doesn't bring improvement? 


Student: I don't know... pollution? But I…


Professor: No, that's good. It would be hard to say pollution was a form of improving the world. 


Student: But isn't pollution just a side effect of… Oh. It kind of goes against the grand narrative about technology, it complicates things. 


Professor: Yeah. And that's a basic idea behind post-modernism. That we should be skeptical of grand narratives, because there's a good chance, they're not completely true. 


Student: You mean?


Professor: Not to say there is no truth in grand narratives of course. It's just that nothing is as simple and straightforward as it seems. We should look critically at the things we assume. 


Student: Okay, I think I get it. Thanks.

Listen to a conversation between a student and a professor.


P: I'm going to suggest you rewrite this paper, Ron.


S: Not bad, uh. 


P: Actually not bad at all. Your paper is very well written, but it has a major problem, one that we need to talk about.


S: Ok?


P: The novel you wrote about, The Wide Sargasso Sea, you know that's based on another novel, right?


S: Yeah, it's based on Jane Eyre.


P: Right. So why didn't you discuss Jane Eyre in your paper?


S: Well, I've never read Jane Eyre, but I also think that The Wide Sargasso Sea can stand on its own.


P: I don't think anyone would dispute that. Jean Rhys is a wonderful author, and if you were just reading the book for fun... But if you want to write an academic paper on this book, well, I don't think you can do that without acknowledging Jane Eyre.


S: I'm not sure,  I mean...because really isn't every book that's ever been written influenced by other books? So like...it's difficult to know where to draw the line.


P: But we're not talking about subtle influences in this case. The issue here is a very concrete case of one writer basing her book directly on another book, and a very famous one like that.


S: But Jean Rhys didn't just rewrite Jane Eyre, did she?


P: If you'd read Jane Eyre, you'd know that what Ryths did was, in a sense, she reinvented it by writing it from the point of view of a secondary character in Jane Eyre. Antoinette, the main character that you wrote so much about in your paper, she's just a minor character in Jane Eyre, someone we hear about, but hardly ever see. So do you think that Ryths would have written The Wide Sargasso Sea if Jane Eyre had never been written?


S: I don't know. I mean, I saw a lot of parallels between Antoinette and Jean Rhys. They were both born in the West Indies, and then they moved to England as young women. Maybe Ryths would have written a similar book anyway.


P: Then why didn't she just base Antoinette on her own life? Why use a character someone else invented? And isn't there more to the setting than just where the author happens to come from? Don't you think there's more to the fact that Jane Eyre takes place in England, but The Wide Sargasso Sea is mostly set in Jamaica?


S: It's just that I've already worked so hard and my schedule is so tight. Now, I wish I'd scheduled a conference with you before I started my paper.


P: Well I hold those conferences for a reason. Look, what you wrote is good. It's very thoughtful. You make some really original points. And I guess that's why I'd like to see you take another stab at this. Right now you'll get an okay grade, but I think if you just read Jane Eyre you could write something really special, at least think about it.


S: Ok, but reading another book and rewriting the paper...even if I can find the time, I'm not sure I've got anything creative left to say.
