Listen to part of a lecture in a marine biology class


Today, we're going to start talking about the way marine life forms have adapted to survive in the ocean. Even when we look at some of the smaller, less well known organisms(生物), what we'll see are some really fascinating adaptations. For example, let's start with a group of aquatic(水生的) plants known as sea grasses. (1)


Sea grasses grow underwater, mostly in the ocean, and they look a lot like terrestrial(陆地上的) grass, the grass we have on land. But be careful with this. Sea grasses are actually not closely related to those grasses, so don't pay too much attention to the name. In fact, they are not even closely related to the other type of marine plant, algae. Sea grasses belong to a group called angiosperms. Angiosperms are flowering plants, and many of the angiosperms were familiar with live on land. There are plants that have flowers, like roses, sunflowers. Anything that has flowers as part of its reproductive(繁殖的) system is an angiosperms, which is tricky, since this includes organisms that don't flower so prominently, that flower more rarely, that look nothing like flowers, like sea grass. (2)


Now, an ocean environment is radically different from a terrestrial environment, so the question is, what are the adaptations that allowed the sea grasses, these angiosperms, to thrive in the ocean? Let's start with photosynthesis. Photosynthesis, as we've learned, is how plants, marine and terrestrial plants, produce nutrients(营养物)  their food. Basically, the process requires sunlight, water and carbon dioxide. For sea grasses, getting enough light and carbon dioxide are big problems. 


OK. So, light. A lot of light, is lost as it travels deeper into the ocean. It gets dark quickly the deeper underwater you get. Although sea grasses often grow in shallow areas, they've also evolved to be very efficient at capturing the light that reaches them. For example, in land plants, most photosynthesis can occur only on the top side of leaves. For sea grasses, photosynthesis can occur on both sides of the leaves. (3) Each blade of sea grass is technically a leaf. Anyway, the result is that they're able to perform more photosynthesis with less light. 


I also mentioned carbon dioxide, and this is a big problem in the ocean too. In water, carbon dioxide dissolves(溶解)  easily and gets broken down into compounds. Sea grasses need carbon dioxide, but it's not directly available. Only these dissolved compounds are available. So sea grasses do something unexpected. They can actually take one of these dissolved compounds by carbonate and process it back into carbon dioxide to use in photosynthesis. This is a very unusual adaptation. (4)


Apart from photosynthesis, of course, sea grasses have had to adapt to their environment in other ways. The structure of their roots helps to anchor(使固定) them securely in the sea floor. Even the flexible blades of the sea grass are advantageous. With all that wave action, rigid plant stems could snap and break. 


Some of these adaptations are quite extensive. I mean, think about how much salt is present in ocean water. As I said earlier, sea grass resembles the grass you might find growing in a field or a park. But imagine watering the grass in a park with salt water. It's not going to respond too well. But seagrass is immersed in salt water constantly. That's a totally different environment. So it clearly is using some pretty complex adaptations to be able to tolerate salt. We don't have time for all the details today, and actually, experts are still making new discoveries. It's possible there are multiple processes involved. (5)


What we can say is that when we compare sea grass and terrestrial grass, we're talking about two very different organisms which have adjusted to their environments in very different ways. For any plant though, aquatic or terrestrial, there's also the challenge of surviving in an environment with the numerous herbivores(食草动物). For example, sea grasses have adapted to marine predators by evolving a horizontal plant stem that is located under the layer of sediment(沉积物) on the sea floor. It's the core of the plant, and since it's located underground, it's hard for predators to damage. (6) Even if herbivores eat the top part of the sea grass, the most vital part of the plant, the stem, is able to survive and regrow. Actually, their defenses against predation are quite similar to those developed by many grasses on land.
