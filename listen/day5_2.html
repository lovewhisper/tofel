Listen to part of a lecture in an archaeology class. 


It's a popular image of archaeology, the machete-wheeling explorers cutting and chopping their way through a wilderness in search of lost civilizations. Well, they often do need machetes when they're traversing a rainforest, but it's not as glamorous as it looks in the movies and not as easy. Finding and mapping ancient sites is slow, tedious(冗长的), sweaty work. (2)


[Couldn't they just fly over the rainforest in an airplane and take photographs?] 


There are certainly advantages to viewing a site from above. However, if the site is thickly covered with vegetation, aerial photography doesn't provide enough detail, because the view is blocked by the trees. It wouldn't be much use in areas covered by rainforest. Fortunately, we do have mapping technology that can reveal structures hidden below the vegetation. It's called Lidar, which stands for light detection and ranging. (1) (3)


Here a lidar works, a pilot flies a small plane back and forth over a site in a grid pattern. While flying, the plane beams a stream of laser signals at the ground below. The signals bounce back(反弹). The amount of time it takes for each signal to bounce back is automatically recorded. What does that tell us? Well, variations(差异) in these bounce back times indicate variations in the elevation of the surface below. 


The plane also contains a GPS device. GPS device links with a global positioning system, that's a network of space satellites that uses radio waves to identify locations on earth. The plane’s GPS device uses information from the satellites to record the position and orientation(方向) of each lidar signal that bounces back. The results are analyzed by a computer, and what you end up with is a map that shows all the structures on the surface, even if they're concealed under abundant plant growth. It looks very much like a three dimensional(3D) map. 


[Are you saying that lidar can see right through leaves? ]


The signals don't actually penetrate through foliage, but there are enough tiny gaps between these where the signals get through. But let's see a site has a smooth covering with no gaps at all, like a layer of volcanic ash, for instance. Lidars are no use at a site like that, nor does it work at sites where the weather is always wet. Laser signals don't travel through rain, snow or fog. 


Plus, it's got to be expensive. The plane and the equipment and everything. 


Well, that's true, of course, but in terms of what it delivers, lidar can change our understanding of a whole civilization almost overnight. (6) Here's an example. There's a site in Belize called Caracol. It was built centuries ago by the Mayas civilization. Over the course of time, Caracol was completely overgrown(覆盖) by rainforest. A group of archeologists began exploring it in the 1980s using the old fashioned methods, walking through the forest. It appeared to be a small place that was occasionally used for public ceremonies. Then in 2009, these archaeologists decided to give Lidar a try. At the end of the mere three weeks, BAM! They had all the evidence they needed to show that Caracol, in the year 650 C.E, was a metropolis with the population of more than 100,000. (4)


[100,000 people! Is that even possible for that era? How did everyone have enough to eat? ]


Because Caracol wasn't just a city, it was a brilliantly design, comprehensive land use system. The inhabitants(居民) lived in well spaced towns that were connected to the city center by a network of roadways. Every bit of land that could be used for growing food was put to use. (5-D) Between the towns were fields. They were laid out as terraces running horizontally across entire hillsides, an excellent way to retain water and stop erosion. And because the Maya collected water during the rainy season, especially built reservoirs(水库), They were able to grow food year round and get multiple harvests out of the fields. (5-B)


And the archaeologists had missed all of that, even the terraces(梯田)? 


Yes, because the ruins of these structures are completely covered by vegetation. They all look like part of the terrain. 


[So are they going to go back and do light or mapping of all the ancient sites? ]


Yes, if it's feasible, because without it, we're trying to draw conclusions from inadequate information. As for the cost, once lidars are done, it shows archaeologists exactly where to dig, so they don't waste their time or their money poking around the wrong places. (6)

文本：


Listen to part of a lecture in an environmental science class 


Professor: Ok. So environmentalists have created green building programs in order to encourage green building construction, which means environmentally friendly buildings that produce a minimum of pollution and are energy efficient. 

教授：因此，环保主义者制定了绿色建筑计划，以鼓励绿色建筑建设，绿色建筑也就是污染小而且节能的环境友好型建筑。


Female Student: We’ve talked about like technologies that use solar energy for heating homes.

女生：我们已经讨论过利用太阳能为家庭供暖的技术。


Professor: Yes. That’s certainly something these programs to advocate. But they are also concerned with construction, because green building standards include the conservation of natural resources like trees, wood home such as those traditionally built here in the United States require over an acre of trees, over an acre of forest land in lumber. 

教授：是的。这当然是这些计划所提倡的。但他们也关心建筑，因为绿色建筑标准涉及到对树木等自然资源进行保护，美国传统的木屋需要超过一英亩的树木，超过一英亩的林地。


Plus three to seven tons of waste are created on average during the construction process. So what if we could use a product that's typically considered waste to build homes? It's being done. 

One of the materials used in green building construction is straw bales. (2-A)

此外，施工过程中平均会产生三到七吨废物。那么，如果我们可以使用一种通常被认为是垃圾的产品来建房子呢？我们已经做到了。

绿色建筑施工中使用的材料之一是稻草堆。


Yes, I'm talking straw. Those short stalks that are left over in farm fields after grain crops are harvested. It's hard to get rid of as a waste product and it's usually burned. So instead of adding to air pollution problems by burning the straw, it's compressed into bales and used to build homes. 

是的，我说的是稻草。谷物收获后留在农田中的短茎。它很难作为废物处理掉，通常会被烧掉。因此，秸秆被压缩成捆，用于建造房屋，而不是通过燃烧秸秆来增加空气污染问题。


Male Student: I’ve never heard of using straw to build homes. 

男学生：我从没听说过用稻草建房子。


Professor: Well, straw bale construction isn't a new technique. A pioneers on the American prairie where there were no trees constructed their homes of straw bales over a hundred years ago. And in many of those early buildings made in what's called the Nebraska style are still standing. Actually, straw bell construction has been used around the world for thousands of years. (3)

教授：稻草堆建造并不是一种新技术。一百多年前，美国大草原上一位没有树木的拓荒者用稻草包建造了自己的家园。在许多早期建筑中，内布拉斯加州风格的建筑仍然屹立不倒。事实上，稻草堆建造技术在世界各地已经使用了数千年。


And because of the very properties that make it a nuisance waste, that it's tough and hard to compost, that's what makes straw a great building material. 

正是因为它的特性使其成为令人讨厌的垃圾，而且很难堆肥（腐烂），所以稻草是一种很好的建筑材料。


It's the insulating properties of straw bales that are probably the strongest selling point. In fact, a standard straw bale wall provides about three times the insulation value of an insulated wall in a traditional wood frame house, which can drastically reduce the amount of heating or cooling needed to keep the house comfortable. (2) So environmentalists love this construction method that basically turns a waste product into a building material, saving energy and saving trees. (2-AC) Paul? 

稻草堆的绝缘性能可能它最大的“卖点”。事实上，标准稻草墙提供的隔热值大约是传统木结构房屋隔热墙的三倍，这可以大大减少保持房屋舒适所需的加热或制冷量。所以环保主义者喜欢这种基本上将废物转化为建筑材料、节约能源和树木的施工方法。Paul？


Male Student: How do you make a house out of straw? It almost sounds too good to be true. 

男学生：怎么用稻草做房子呢？这听起来好得难以置信。


Professor: Straw bale houses are actually built out of standard rectangular agricultural bales. Their stack like bricks to form the exterior walls, then the straw walls are sealed with stucco. Stucco is a good tough finish, that's like cement or plaster. These stucco straw bale walls can then be used as the structure to support the roof. (4-A) The stucco skin acts as a thin shell over the bales and makes the buildings very stable. 

教授：草捆屋实际上是用标准的矩形（长方体状）农业稻草捆建造的。它们像砖块一样堆叠形成外墙，然后用灰泥密封稻草墙。灰泥是一种很好的坚硬饰面，就像水泥或灰泥一样。这些涂过灰泥的草捆墙可以用作支撑屋顶的结构。灰泥皮在捆上起到保护层的作用，使建筑物非常稳定。


And there are more green advantages here. The compositive effect of the straw insulation plus the stucco coating can actually create something like a passive solar building. (4-D)

稻草堆建房子还有其他的环保方面优势。秸秆隔热层加上灰泥涂层的综合效应实际上可以创造出一个类似被动太阳能建筑的结构。


Passive solar means that assuming the building is oriented correctly to sun and shade, it can heat and cool itself largely without a modern heating and air conditioning systems that consume a lot of energy. Yes, Jane?

被动式太阳能意味着，假设建筑物正确朝向阳光和阴影（通常就是朝南开窗），它可以在很大程度上自行加热和制冷，而不需要消耗大量能源的现代供暖和空调系统。是的，简？


Female Student: But aren't straw bale houses of fire hazard? I mean straw burns so easily. 

女生：但是稻草屋不是有火灾危险吗？稻草那么容易烧着。


Professor: Well, it turns out that they are actually more fire resistant than the standard wood frame construction. Individual stocks of straw will burn, of course. But when they're compressed into bales, they become fire resistant because oxygen can't circulate through the bale.

教授：事实证明，它们实际上比标准木框架结构更耐火。当然，每一根稻草都会被烧掉。但当它们被压缩成捆时，就会变得耐火，因为氧气不能在稻草捆中循环。


Burning a bale is like trying to burn a dense stack of thick books like telephone directories. An individual page of a telephone directory will burn easily. But a thousand pages tightly pressed together is another story. (5)

烧稻草捆就像试图烧掉一堆厚厚的书，比如电话簿。电话簿的一个单页很容易烧着。但一千页紧紧地压在一起是另一回事。（很难燃烧）


And also the stucco coating prevents oxygen from reaching the bales further reducing its flammability. So is straw bale construction too good to be true? Well, there has been a renewed interest in it worldwide, which is not surprising since from an environmental standpoint, it has very specific advantages in terms of waste, energy and construction processes.

此外，S图层还可以防止氧气进入草捆，从而进一步降低其易燃性。那么，草捆建筑是不是好到有点不太现实了？（哪有这么十全十美的东西的感觉）全世界对草捆建筑重新产生了兴趣，这并不奇怪，因为从环境角度来看，草捆结构在废物、能源和施工工艺方面具有非常独特的优势。


Unfortunately there are drawbacks for home builders in the United States. It isn't a common building technique yet. So there can be difficulties getting your building plans approved. And it can be more expensive than to build a traditional wood frame home. 

不幸的是，稻草捆建筑也存在缺陷。它目前还不是一种普及的建筑技术。因此，稻草捆建筑计划很难获得批准。而且它可能比建造一个传统的木结构房屋更昂贵。


So this is where I think local and national governments may have a role to play. They could encourage the construction of straw bale houses with enticements like tax breaks or reductions on fees for building permits.(6)

因此，我认为地方和国家政府可以在这方面发挥作用。他们可以通过减税或降低建筑许可费等诱惑措施，鼓励建设稻草捆建筑。
