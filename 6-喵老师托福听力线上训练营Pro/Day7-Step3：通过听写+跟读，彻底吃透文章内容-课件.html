Step 3：通过听写+跟读，彻底吃透文章内容

听写练习


听写的目的是找到我们的听力盲区，让我们在做跟读的时候能够更有重点。听写时，每个句子播放一般不要超过三遍（特别长的句子除外），如果听完后依然写不全，可以换行写下一句。 听写完成后比对文本，标清楚没听到、没听懂的地方，以及没听懂的原因（例如单词不认识、单词认识但音不熟悉、语音现象造成的听力困难）。


跟读练习


如果实在没有时间做听写，也请务必依据标注版文本完成跟读。标注版文本如下，橙色为考点，红色为关键词。

逐句跟读音频+文本

（在这部分，我尽可能保留了原文完整的句子/意群，所以有些音频会比较长，也是为了让大家练习听一个完整意群的能力。如果有困难可以自己在中间断一下。）


1.  Listen to part of a lecture in an environmental engineering class.

2.  So, we’ve been talking about fossil fuels and the issues that the world is facing due to our finite supply of fossil fuels, like oil.

3.  Can someone give us a quick summary of how oil is formed?

4.  Well, dead plants and other organisms, they get buried under the earth, under the sea, and they get pushed deep underground by geological forces.

5.  Then they’re heated and pressurized and you get oil.

6.  Yes and of course this process takes millions of years, so today let’s consider the pros and cons of some alternative ways of making oil.

7.  Some of them use recycled waste products to create oil. Yes, recycled garbage.

8.  The early efforts to create oil from waste resulted in the low-quality oil that was not a serious alternative to natural crude oil, but there’s a newer process I want to start with. It’s called thermal depolymerization.

9.  With thermal depolymerization, it only takes hours to create oil and it’s a pretty decent quality.

10.  It uses water, pressure and heat to convert organic material into a variety of useful products including crude oil, which can be refined into gasoline and other oil-derived products and it doesn’t produce any polluting emissions.

11.  Um… I think I remember from the article you assigned that thermal depolymerization can use both organic and inorganic waste as its source, right?

12.  Yes and that’s another big improvement from earlier attempts.

13.  Waste products, almost any kind of waste, both organic and inorganic, can be used as a source.

14.  For example, old tires, plastic bottles, even old household appliances.

15.  You chop up everything into tiny pieces, dump the stuff into a vat and then add water.

16.  Water is an important ingredient because it reduces the amount of heat needed which increases the efficiency of the process.

17.  Remember, if the process isn’t efficient, you’ll end up using nearly as much energy to produce the oil as you’ll get from the oil itself and it won’t be a viable process.

18.  Yeah, that wouldn’t make much sense.

19.  So, after the waste and water are added to the vat, the mixture is ground into a pulp then the mixture is heated.

20.  Now, after that, and this is another feature that previous processes didn’t have… there are two reactor stages.

21.  In the first reactor stage, the mixture is cooked with heat and pressure for about an hour to break apart the molecules that the waste material is composed of.

22. Then the excess water and minerals are removed. 23.  In the second reactor stage, the mixture is heated to 260 degrees Celsius and pressurized to 42 kilograms per square centimeter and in 20 minutes the process replicates what it takes nature thousands, or even millions, of years to do.

24.  Then there are several more steps, which we aren’t gonna discuss right now, but one point I do want to make relates to the efficiency of the process.

25.  Currently, the claim is that only 15% of the energy obtained is used to power the process and 85% of the energy is usable for other purposes. Not bad, huh?

26.  But, what about global warming and the greenhouse effect?

27.  I mean it would be great if we could recycle garbage into a useful product, but that useful product is oil, and as long as we continue to burn oil we’ll continue to pollute the atmosphere with greenhouse gases containing carbon.

28.  So, is this process really a win for the environment in the long run or does it cause environmental damage?

29.  Well, I should mention that research on the thermal depolymerization process has received funding from environmental groups, but you raise a good point.

30.  Proponents of the process claim that we could eventually find enough sources of waste containing carbon to produce oil so that we could eliminate the need for the traditional sources of oil completely.

31.  And therefore the only carbon that we’d use would already be above ground, thus making it a so-called ‘carbon neutral process’.

32.  But, and this is a very big but, I think it’s overly optimistic and perhaps naïve to assume that oil created by the thermal depolymerization process will completely replace traditional oil.

33.  Not only that… if the price of oil were to go down over time, then the demand for oil might increase and we’d actually end up using even more oil than we do now.
