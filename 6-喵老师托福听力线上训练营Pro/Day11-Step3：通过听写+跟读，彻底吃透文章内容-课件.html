Step 3：通过听写+跟读，彻底吃透文章内容


听写练习


听写的目的是找到我们的听力盲区，让我们在做跟读的时候能够更有重点。听写时，每个句子播放一般不要超过三遍（特别长的句子除外），如果听完后依然写不全，可以换行写下一句。 听写完成后比对文本，标清楚没听到、没听懂的地方，以及没听懂的原因（例如单词不认识、单词认识但音不熟悉、语音现象造成的听力困难）。


跟读练习


如果实在没有时间做听写，也请务必依据标注版文本完成跟读。标注版文本如下，橙色为考点，红色为关键词。


逐句跟读音频+文本

（在这部分，我尽可能保留了原文完整的句子/意群，所以有些音频会比较长，也是为了让大家练习听一个完整意群的能力。如果有困难可以自己在中间断一下。）

1.  Listen to part of a lecture in a biology class.

2. Polar bears inhabit the Arctic, on the outer perimeters of the sea, to be exact.

3. When polar bears die, their bones always end up deep down on the floor of the icy Arctic Ocean. They basically disappear forever.

4. Recently, however, a polar bear's jawbone was found within the Arctic Circle, on an island in Norway Svalbard Archipelago.

5. This bone held invaluable information regarding the polar bear's revolutionary (evolutionary?) path.

6. For instance, exactly how long the polar bear has existed as a species has been a matter of wild debate.

7. So researchers took the opportunity to perform dating analyses on the bone, and also to analyze its DNA.

8. The bone was determined to be about 120,000 years old, and the DNA analysis established that it was indeed the bone of a polar bear.

9. Now, in order to map out the polar bear's genetic profile, researchers performed mitochondrial DNA analysis.

10. Usually when we think of DNA, we think of the DNA that's in cell's nucleus, but in fact, a cell mitochondria also contain DNA.

11. In this particular situation, mitochondrial DNA analysis made more sense than nuclear DNA analysis.

12. You remember from a previous lecture that a mitochondrion is the energy producing structure in cells, right?

13. Well, it has a much more stable structure than a nucleus.

14. That's a distinct advantage when you're investigating ancient delicate samples.

15. Additionally, there're up to 1,000 mitochondria within a single cell, but there are only two copies of nuclear DNA within a cell.

16. This abundance of samples makes mitochondrial DNA analysis a much easier procedure.

17. As far as genetics and ancestry are concerned, we've long been aware that polar bears are young species, evolutionarily speaking, and that they branched off from brown bears.

18. But this jawbone fossil has helped us better estimate when that splitting off probably occurred.

19. By the way, as part of their investigation, researchers studied a unique geographically isolated group of modern brown bears living in southeastern Alaska.

20. It turns out these brown bears have genes that resembled polar bear genes more than they resembled the genes of other brown bears.

21. Anyway, the jawbone fossil appears to have come from a polar bear that had lived shortly after, well, historically speaking that is, shortly after polar bears agreed to split into a different species.

22. And what does all this mean? Well, because the fossil is so old, polar bears must have survived Earth's last interglacial warming period, about 45,000 years ago.

23. But polar bears are Arctic animals and live on the ice. In that unique environment, how exactly did they manage?

24. Well, one theory suggests that polar bears may have been able to find a few remote cold icy places, such as, well, Svalbard Islands to live on until the climate eventually cooled again.

25. Controversially, because the last warming period was actually warmer than the one now, the one more presently experiencing, some people believe that polar bears will be able to pull through again.

26. But there's something they are not taking into account, namely, the pace at which climate change is occurring today.

27. The climate today is becoming warmer at a very accelerated rate. Polar bears are highly specialized species.

28. It is unrealistic to expect them to be able to adapt in such a short window of time.

29. Incidentally, the climate isn't the only factor that might have an effect on the polar bear's future.

30. Recently the offspring of a polar bear and a grizzly bear was discovered in the southern Arctic.

31. This hybrid was a result of a polar bear and a grizzly bear mating.

32. You see, it seems that grizzly bears in Canada have been moving northward, intruding on the polar bear's territory.

33. This is exactly the first time that we've seen such a hybrid in the wild, but it's possible that hybridization could occur more and more frequently, resulting in increasing numbers of offspring that can no longer be considered true polar bears. And if that pattern continued over time, well… [END]
