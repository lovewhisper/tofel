Listen to part of a lecture in an art history class.



Okay. Let's look at two specific ancient cultures of the arctic, both of which lived on the far northern edge of the continent, the Dorset and the Thule. The Dorset culture began around 800 BC and ranged across most of arctic Canada. The Thule, well, they lived in the same area, but about 1200 years later. Both groups left some beautiful art behind, really our best evidence that these folks even existed. And although these two cultures lived in the same region, each group approach their art in very different ways. 


Alright, so the Dorset culture. (1) Archaeologists have unearthed hundreds of Dorset sculptures, mostly carved from ivory or bone, and sometimes stone. They were mostly small sculptures of animal or human figures, and our best guess is that they were probably used mainly for religious purposes. (3-1 )The main subject of Dorset animal sculptures is the polar bear. Some of these polar bears are represented in naturalistic or realistic ways, but usually they are abstracted or stylized, that is to say, less realistic. Here's a typical polar bear sculpture. It's carved from ivory. We call these artifacts "flying" or "floating" polar bears because they look like the polar bears doing just that, flying or floating. Notice the distinctive markings, they seem to mark out the skeleton, or joints of the bear. While polar bears were a favorite subject, the Dorset created sculptures of other animals, too, like seals and caribou, which were represented in a more realistic fashion. 


We speculate that if the Dorset feared or respected a particular animal, they were more likely to present it in a stylized way.  Like with the polar bear, which of course is a large, dangerous predator, maybe it was a way of dealing with their fear. (2) We also have Dorset sculptures whose meaning is more elusive. I'm talking about horns with many human faces carved into them. What's remarkable about these sculptures is the variety of facial types and expressions that appear on a single horn. Ah… If only the people represented on these artifacts could answer our questions, tell us whether they are ancestors or residents of a village or what. Okay, let's switch to Thule art, which differs from Dorset art in in some important ways. Interestingly, Thule artwork wasn't generally influenced by the earlier Dorset culture. It seems to have grown out of a separate tradition that originated in Alaska. Thule artwork was most likely used to decorate common objects such as tools with attractive designs. (4) We can't be entirely sure about this, however, we've tried to interpret the symbolic designs in Thule art, but of course it's impossible to confirm the accuracy of such interpretations. What we do know is that the Thule people made extensive use of engravings. They drilled small holes and carve lines into various materials. These engravings, engraved patterns, rather, are found on a diverse group of artifacts, things ranging from weapons to jewelry to combs and boxes. Here's some examples. It's hard to see, but most of these objects do have engravings on them. Thule art was more of a graphic art, uh more like drawing. Many of their designs are geometric and abstract. Others contain engravings of human and animal figures. For instance, the long, skinny object on the left has a face at the top. We've also found a tool handle with hunting and camp scenes carved into it. 


[As far as Thule sculpture goes, ] there's only a few small objects and wooden dolls, like the one in the bottom right hand corner. These have either no facial features or very rudimentary ones, less detailed, less individualized than the faces of Dorset sculpture. One scholar has argued that this difference resulted from the nature of Thule society, which was built around group hunting expeditions on the ocean. This hunting approach required a great deal of cooperation, which may have led to a kind of an overall uniformity in their art. An emphasis on commonality rather than individuality, which probably explains why there are human figures lacked faces or had standardized faces that all looked similar. (5) The most famous of the few Thule sculptures are the ones of birds and creatures that are part bird and part woman. While these sculptures probably had some symbolic purpose, patterns of holes were drilled into them too. So in general, compared with the artwork of the Dorset, Thule art probably had a more decorative or utilitarian function. ③-2/④-2

Student: Well It's a mineral. Um I know it has unusual properties, but… 


Professor: Great. And no, that's a good start. Zircon is a particularly sturdy mineral. It forms crystals. And the thing about zircon is when it forms crystals, these crystals encapsulate or grow around other materials. So the zircon crystals retain evidence of the conditions that were present when they formed. And even the other materials that were present at the time. (3)


Now researchers in the 1980s discovered some zircon crystals in Australia. At that time, though, we lacked the technology to make any definite claims about the materials found in the zircon. There seemed to be some evidence of material from way, way back, before scientists thought earth had cooled enough for it to exist. But researchers didn't have any conclusive evidence at the time. (4)


Student: So they still thought earth started cooling when it was 500 million years old. 


Professor: Well the discovery of those zircon crystals was pretty exciting, you know, but, really, most scientists had to stick with the theory that had the clearest evidence. And like I said, technology just wasn't at the point yet where it could really challenge the accepted view.


Student: But how did they date the zircon and the material in it? 


Professor: Well in the 1980s, they used um a special kind of ion microprobe. So with this special ion microprobe, they removed a few atoms from the zircon surface. Then they compared the mass of those atoms. That's how they measure the composition of the atoms. And this is what enabled the researchers to date the zircons. In fact, using this technique, they could date specific parts of a single crystal of zircon. And then years later, in 1999, they began to reinvestigate with an improved ion microprobe. They analyzed another group of crystals and found that a few of them were more than 4 billion years old, which means they were around during those first 500 million years when we thought earth was a very fiery place that destroyed any rocks or crystals that came in contact with it. 


Student: But for some reason, the zircon crystals weren't destroyed? 


Professor: That's right. I mean we know zircon can withstand a lot, but it definitely couldn't have survived a constant fireball. So the question was: were these zircon crystals an indication that earth started cooling earlier than we thought? Out of those crystals they were looking at the oldest one seemed to have been around when earth was only 100 million years old. If our view of early earth was correct, that shouldn't be possible. Okay now here's where it gets interesting. They did some further testing on these zircon crystals. Remember how I said zircons contain evidence, not just of materials, but also what the conditions were like when they formed? They found that the rock that was crystallized inside the oldest zircons had high oxygen isotope ratios. 


Student: How do zircons and rocks get high oxygen isotope ratios? 


Professor: Um… The required conditions are — and get this — liquid water and low temperatures. (5) This is the only known way that high oxygen isotope ratios can occur in zircons and rocks, yep, water and low temperatures. So… 


Student: So, and that's when earth was only 100 million years old? That's really different from uh…


Professor: Uh *very* different, so we're really had to rethink our ideas of the conditions on earth back then. (6)

In the first year we work there. In *one week* we recorded *77* undisturbed sites. That's *phenomenal*. I've worked in archaeology pretty much all my adult life. And I’ve only seen half a dozen sites that I knew were absolutely undisturbed. But in Range Creek Canyon, there are *hundreds*. (2)


Student: Like what kind of stuff have you found there?


Professor: Oh. Houses, granaries, rock art, uh digging sticks, ropes, stone blades, arrows and arrowheads, lots of pottery, what looks like personal jewelry? And we’ve only surveyed *oh maybe* 5 to 10 percent of the area. It's mind-boggling.
